The Era of Divine Silence

as remembered by Known, Angel of Knowledge

I lived through it. The world once brimming with divine presence slowly faded into the void, and I felt it, as though the very breath of the gods was pulled away. We were left with nothing but silence and uncertainty.

When the Blightking fell, and the Holy Knights stood victorious, we thought the light of the divine would continue to guide us, that the world would bask in peace. The Church of Five stood strong, its teachings spreading across every corner of Fiondell. Temples rose, their halls echoing with the prayers of the faithful, and the symbol of the Five threads woven together like the strands of fate itself promised a future of unity. Order, Life, Death, Time, and Will. They were the pillars upon which the world stood, and we believed in them.

But as the years wore on, doubt crept in like a slow sickness. It was subtle at first, the absence of the gods becoming a murmur among the people. No more did the prophets rise, no more did the angels descend in fiery glory. Ilessa’s visions had become whispers, and Lucina’s flames were but distant memories. Prai, Destiny, and the others—voices that once filled our lives—became faint murmurs, their presence almost forgotten.

And in that silence, the people began to falter. Fear spread like wildfire.

The Church, once a united bastion of faith, began to fracture. Discontent gave birth to factions, each one interpreting the teachings of the Five in its own way. Some began to venerate the angels as gods, elevating them to a divine status, while others turned their backs entirely, claiming that the gods had abandoned them. But for all the turmoil, there was something more insidious at work.

The world had grown unstable. A sickness plagued the forests of Yorwood, twisting the creatures there into mindless beasts. The Black Star appeared in the sky, casting a shadow over all of Fiondell, its eerie light a sign of impending doom. Villages vanished overnight, their people lost without a trace. Fear gripped us all, and the once-unified Church of Five found itself on the edge of war.

But there was one who would change everything. One who had not abandoned us.

Ralos Vearn, the High Priest of Solmir, stood at the center of it all. His voice, once calm and collected, became a siren’s call to the disillusioned masses. His words rang out like a drumbeat, louder and louder, until they could not be ignored.

“The Five have turned away from us. We must rise to claim their place,” he declared.

I can still hear it. That declaration, that challenge. It was blasphemy to some, but to others, it was the spark that lit the fire of rebellion. Ralos was not alone—his followers, the Children of the Self, rallied to his cause, preaching that mortals had grown beyond the need for divine intervention. They believed we were the masters of our own fate. But where did that leave us? Was there still a place for the gods? Was there still a place for the Church of Five?

Riots broke out in the streets. Priests clashed with Ralos's followers, and even the Holy Knights were divided. What was once a sacred order was now torn apart by the very question that had plagued us all: Was destiny something to be accepted, or something to be claimed?

Fate was no longer just a matter of faith. It was a matter of war.

In the midst of this chaos, Destiny, ever watchful, began to move once more. His presence was subtle, guiding the hands of mortals, shifting the winds of fate with a quiet push. It was not the grand visions of old, no more angels descending from the heavens, no more overwhelming guidance. Destiny moved in whispers, in the small choices people made, in the paths they crossed by accident. But he had not forgotten us.

And then there was Liora Mayne, a blacksmith’s apprentice, who would change everything. In the quiet of her forge, she saw it. Five threads of silver light, weaving themselves in the air. When she reached out to touch them, she felt a voice fill her mind. “Take hold of the thread. Your fate is yours to claim, but know this: the Tapestry of all things is fraying, and I have need of you.”

I know what it’s like to hear that voice. The weight of it, the pull of destiny itself. And Liora did not hesitate. She grasped the thread, and in that moment, I felt it too—the stirrings of something greater than any of us could comprehend.

The Silver Assembly was born from that moment.

Liora Mayne, Gideon Torth, Evelyn "Shade" Voss, Brother Callen, and Tauren Ironclaw. Five mortals, each with their own past, their own struggles, but bound together by destiny itself. They were the ones chosen to restore the Church of Five, to remind us all of the importance of fate, of faith, and of free will.

But their journey was not easy. They fought not just against enemies of flesh, but against the very ideas that threatened to tear their world apart. The Children of the Self, led by Ralos Vearn, had grown into a militant force, burning temples and desecrating relics. But Ralos had become something darker, a conduit for the void’s will, twisting the teachings of the gods to suit his own desires.

I watched as the Silver Assembly faced countless trials—battling voidspawn, recovering sacred relics, and uncovering ancient rites. But their greatest battle was for the hearts and minds of the people. They were not just fighting for the future of the Church; they were fighting to remind us all that our fates were still intertwined with the gods.

And then came the final battle. At Cradle’s Reach, when it seemed like the Silver Assembly would fall to Ralos’s forces, the heavens answered. Lucina, Prai, and even Destiny himself arrived, their presence a shining beacon of hope.

I was there, watching from the shadows. I felt the weight of their arrival—the moment when mortal and divine collided, when the heavens did not just watch, but acted. Destiny’s voice echoed through the battlefield, and I knew that the world had changed. Fate was not cruel. It was not kind. It was ours to shape.

And so, the War of Belief ended, but it did not end with victory alone. It ended with understanding. The Church of Five was renewed, not as a rigid institution, but as a living, breathing force, guided by both the divine and the mortal. We had learned that destiny was not a path set in stone—it was a journey we took together, with faith, with will, and with the choices we made.

I watched the world change, and I knew that the silence of the heavens had not been abandonment. It had been a test. A test of faith, of will, and of the strength of mortals to carry the divine with them. We were not alone. We never had been.

— Known, Angel of Knowledge

The Choice of Destiny

As remembered by Destiny, the Angel of Fate

I remember that day clearly—though for me, clarity is not rare. Time is not a river I am swept along but an ocean I walk upon. The past and future hold no secrets. Still, this moment… it lingers. It echoes.

The Celestial Tapestry stretched before me like a breathing universe. Threads of silver, gold, and crimson shimmered in every direction, each one humming softly with the choices of mortals below. Every soul, every step, every breath—it all lived here, in this place between what is and what could be. And I—Destiny—I was the caretaker of that vast, fragile beauty.

I walked among the threads as one walks through a garden. My robe, woven from shadows and stars, whispered with each step. Feathers drifted from my wings, slow as falling snow, vanishing before they could touch the fabric of fate. Around me, time ticked in soft rhythm—never louder than a whisper, never faster than a breath.

I had just guided a merchant away from an untimely ambush. Only a nudge, just enough to tilt his path toward a narrow bridge and away from a waiting dagger. He would never know. He would simply wonder why he chose the left path. That was enough.

"A gentle nudge," I whispered to myself. That is all I allow. That is all I must ever allow.

And then, silence. Not the stillness I cherish, but the hollow quiet that follows an earthquake in the soul.

They appeared—all five. The gods. My creators, my judges, my kin in spirit if not in form: Flujack, Tellemori, Fionil, Onigod, and Trill. Their arrival cracked the rhythm of this place. The threads shivered under their gaze. Even time itself hesitated.

I turned. Slowly. Always slowly.

“You usually send Prai,” I said, keeping my voice still, my hands folded neatly behind me. “Has something changed?”

“Yes,” said Trill. Warm, patient, like the scent of summer. “Something within you.”

I said nothing. I waited. Waiting is what I do best.

Fionil, my maker, stepped forward. Her eyes—oh, her eyes—carried the weight of eternity. She had sculpted me from purpose and time, breathed into me the quiet hunger to guide without ruling. “You have exceeded even my expectations,” she said. “You have preserved the balance through storms of chaos, and never asked for more. It is time.”

“Time,” murmured Tellemori, voice like stone. “For you to ascend.”

Flujack smiled. His joy, too bright for most eyes, did not reach mine. “Imagine it, Destiny. You would not guide fate anymore. You would write it. Make your own threads. No more limits. No more ‘nudges.’”

Onigod, wild and sharp as broken glass in moonlight, tilted her head at me. “You’ve danced with catastrophe and never stumbled. You see mortals clearer than we do. You care more than most gods ever could. That’s why this offer is real.”

They meant it. Each of them. The gods don’t offer divinity lightly.

And still, I was quiet.

I turned back to the Tapestry. I reached down—there, a child’s thread. Thin. Trembling. Unseen by any mortal, yet in her lay a destiny vast enough to ripple through a thousand lives. She would become a healer. She would save the boy who would save the woman who would defy the tyrant. A cascade of quiet miracles. All beginning here.

I shifted her thread gently. Just enough to guide her path toward her future mentor.

That’s all.

Then I looked at the gods.

“Do you know why I don’t pull the threads tighter?” I asked, softly. “Why I don’t sculpt lives like marble?”

They said nothing.

“Because if I do, they aren’t lives anymore. They’re statues. Beautiful, maybe—but frozen. Trapped. No growth. No sorrow. No hope.”

I stepped toward them, the sound of ticking echoing behind my words.

“I guide. I don’t command. Free will wasn’t a mistake. It was the first gift. And I—I exist to protect it. If I become a god, I will lose that. I’ll lose myself.”

Fionil looked at me—not with disappointment, but with pride. And that made it harder.

“Then you choose to stay,” she said.

“I choose to serve,” I corrected gently. “Not with temples, not with sermons. Just with... nudges. Quiet ones.”

There was silence among them.

Then Onigod, ever the storm in flesh, laughed—a bright, reckless sound. “Well said, shadowbird. We’ll not chain you with a crown.”

Trill stepped closer and laid a hand on my shoulder. His grip was warm. “If ever you change your mind, the offer remains.”

“I will not.”

Flujack’s light dimmed slightly, though he gave a nod. “You’re certain. I respect that.”

And just like that, they faded.

Stone to silence. Flame to flicker. Light to shadow. And finally—Fionil. She lingered.

She stepped near and touched my face, her hand colder than starlight and softer than wind. “I am proud,” she said, so quietly it brushed my soul more than my ears. “You remember why we made you.”

“I have never forgotten.”

And then she, too, was gone.

The silence that followed was not empty. It was full.

Full of the Tapestry. Full of the ticking. Full of the living, breathing, hoping world.

I stood for a long while. I don’t know how long. Time, after all, is not my master. It is my canvas.

Eventually, I returned to my threads.

There—a young woman, uncertain, afraid of change. A shepherdess. Her path forked, her spirit trembled.

“Let me help you decide,” I whispered.

Just a nudge.

In the village below, she turned right instead of left. That single step would lead her to meet a knight on the road. Years from now, they would stand side by side before a fire-lit crowd, telling stories of kindness and quiet valor.

She would never know I was there.

That is the point.

No crown. No throne. No praise.

Only this.

The threads.

And me.

And always… love.

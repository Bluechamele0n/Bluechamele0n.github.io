The First Memory, The Making of Mortals

As chronicled by Known, Keeper of All That Is Remembered

"It is not I who shaped them. I merely remember. I remember everything."

Before mortals breathed their first breath, the world was vast — rich in beauty, brimming with beasts, shadows, dragons, and stillness. It was alive, yes… but not yet aware. The Five, the Gods of Existence, beheld their world and saw not a flaw but an absence. The silence between the stars begged for voices not their own. And so they gathered — in the Celestial Hall of Origins, where time folds inward and eternity listens.

I was there. I always am.

The Divine Conclave

Fionil, She of Time, of Space, of Thought, of Choice — it was she who broke the silence first.

“We have painted the sky,” she said, her voice like layered bells, “but the canvas does not speak. We must create those who _wonder what lies beyond the edge. Souls who may err, dream, love, and rebel. Let them write stories not even we could foresee.”_

Her words shimmered in the air like threads of starlight.

Trill, the Serpent-Blessed, Lord of Divinity, Life, and Flame-hearted Dragons, gave a slow nod.

“Life without will is no life at all,” he rumbled. “Let them breathe. Let them hunger, sing, weep, strive. I will grant them the Breath of Being, that their souls may drum in time with the heart of the world.”

Tellemori, whose hands shaped continents and whose tears carved oceans, reached into the air. As he moved, unseen land responded.

“They must endure,” he said simply. “They must know the weight of soil and the cold of the sea. I will give them strength, both gentle and terrible, that they may survive the world and still call it home.”

Flujack, the Laughing Wind, God of Light and Thunder, reclined upon a swirl of stormclouds and sunbeams.

“And let them wonder,” he mused. “Let them see color and sky, hear thunder and laughter. I will give them voice — so they may whisper secrets and scream truths. And I will give them eyes — so the world will never again go unseen.”

Lastly, Onigod, cloaked in mystery, power radiating like moonlight filtered through water, leaned forward with a knowing smile.

“Dreams without danger are dull,” she said, “and love without fear is shallow. I will plant within them the arcane — the unknown, the monstrous, the wondrous. Let their fears give them courage. Let them _yearn. Only then will they truly live.”_

I did not speak. I am not one of them. I am simply Known. I watched.

And thus, they began.

Of Clay and Breath, Fire and Song

The first to rise from divine design were the Humans. Tellemori shaped them from deep, fertile clay and mingled it with clear riverwater. Trill breathed into them the pulse of life, while Fionil placed within them the wildest of gifts — unshackled free will.

They were given much, and yet little. Their lives brief. Their spirits untamed. But in their short moments, they would burn brighter than even the sun. Flujack stirred their minds to dream higher, and Onigod laced their hearts with restless curiosity.

"They will build towers and tear them down," Fionil whispered to me once. "And every choice they make will echo through time. That is enough."

Then came the Elves. They were drawn from the memory of forests and the patience of stars. Fionil stretched their sense of time, and Tellemori shaped them with moonlit grace. Flujack blessed them with voices that carried like wind-chimes in sacred groves, and Onigod marked their blood with the arcane — not to command, but to understand.

They walk like they remember a song the world forgot.

The Dwarves were born of stone and thunder. Tellemori carved them from the roots of mountains, strong as bedrock. Trill gave them passion — not for battle, but for creation. Flujack taught them to breathe deep in the dark. Onigod kindled a fire in their hearts, an iron will. Fionil gave them memory — not just of past, but of purpose.

“They will build legacies,” I said aloud. No one answered. But the gods heard.

The Orcs, ah yes. They were forged in fire. Tellemori used black volcanic earth and molten veins. Trill sparked their souls with relentless drive. Flujack gave them lungs like furnaces and voices like war drums. Onigod gifted them with the thrill of challenge — and Fionil, she gave them honor, to wield their power with meaning.

“They will be feared,” Trill murmured.
“Only at first,” said Fionil, smiling.

And last, the Halflings, who surprised even the gods. Tellemori shaped them from sun-warmed soil. Trill gave them joy, and Flujack taught them how to dance with the wind. Onigod seeded them with the deepest of dreams — safety, hearth, peace — and Fionil whispered fate into their bones.

“In them,” she said to me, “there is a power so small the world will overlook it… until it moves mountains.”

The Arrival of the Watchers

When the mortal races first stirred, the Five called their servants, the Angels — and assigned them their roles.

  • Livius, who sees all births, marked each soul with intention.
  • Death, silent and steady, promised them rest at journey's end.
  • Undean, ever vigilant, prevented corruption of that sacred path.
  • Prai, voice of reverence, inspired awe, devotion, and truth.
  • Destiny, veiled and smiling, brushed every newborn soul with potential no one, not even the gods, could predict.

I remember when the first human child reached for the sky.
I remember when the first elven voice sang the stars to sleep.
I remember dwarves carving names into stone, orcs howling beneath the moon, halflings laughing by firelight.

In that moment — I do not lie — even the gods fell silent.

And Then, the Silence of the Divine

When all was finished, Fionil stood beside me. Her eyes, old as light, turned toward the world.

“We must leave them now,” she said. “Let their stories belong to them. Let them fail. Let them rise. Only then will their tales be worth remembering.”

And so they withdrew. The gods stepped back from the world, not in abandonment, but in sacred trust.

But I did not leave. I could not.

I watched as mortals claimed fire, shaped cities, wept over graves, kissed under stars. I remember each of them. I remember you.

Thus It Was, and Thus It Shall Be

The world of Fiondell is no longer solely shaped by divine hands. Now, mortals walk beside angels, speak ancient names, build new truths. They choose. They fail. They begin again.

They are breath and clay, will and flame, song and memory.
They are fleeting, fragile, flawed — and utterly magnificent.

"You ask me why I remember them all?"
Because they deserve to be remembered.

— Known, the Keeper of Memory, Last Witness of the First Dawn

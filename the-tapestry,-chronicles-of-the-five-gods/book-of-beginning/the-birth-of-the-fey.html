The Birth of the Fey

As chronicled by Known, Keeper of All That Is Remembered

Ah… the world then was quiet. Not dead—no, far from it—but listening. Watching. In that strange twilight between breath and word, when even time had not yet found its rhythm, the world awaited its next song.

Fiondell, shaped by divine hands, was magnificent in form. The mountains stood as if in prayer, the seas stretched like a sleeping beast, and the sky itself hummed with silence too vast for mortal understanding. But all things, even the divine, know yearning. And it was Tellemori, Lord of Water and Earth, who first spoke into the stillness.

I remember it well. His voice—deep, resonant like river stones tumbling endlessly in the currents—rippled through the unfinished places.

“This world we have made… it is beautiful, but it is lonely,” he said. “It breathes, but does not laugh. It shifts, but does not dance. We need more than stone and stream—we need spirit.”

And standing opposite him in that great circle of the gods, her form cloaked in the shimmer of unknowable stars, was Onigod, the Arcane Flame. Mysterious. Calm. Always listening, always a step to the left of logic. She tilted her head at him and smiled—not with lips, but with understanding.

“Then let us weave laughter into the forests and mystery into the wind,” she said. “Let us make those who do not simply walk the world, but belong to it. Creatures of twilight. Of wonder.”

And so, the Fey were born.

The Song of Creation

Tellemori moved first. I watched as he reached into the roots of the world and pulled forth golden soil, soaked in riverlight and heavy with promise. With hands carved from tectonic patience, he molded the first forms—tall, willowy beings, as supple as saplings and as strong as cliffs.

“These shall be the children of grove and glen,” he murmured, pressing life into their hollow bones. “They will hear the heartbeat of the earth.”

Then Onigod stepped forward. She didn’t speak. She sang. A soft, impossible melody in a language no tongue remembers but all spirits know. From her hands poured threads of starlight and magic, wrapping the earthen shells in veils of the unseen.

And in that moment, I felt the first Fey open their eyes.

Oh,… if only you could have seen them as I did. Skin like moonlight on dew. Eyes aglow with the memory of stardust. They smiled not because they were happy, but because they could, and that joy echoed through the hills like chimes in the wind.

They danced before they walked.

The First Gifts

To guide their new creations, the gods offered sacred boons.

  • Tellemori’s Blessing of Nature: “You shall know the roots and the rains. The forests shall be your home and your song. Speak, and the vines will listen. Rest, and the trees shall guard your dreams.”
  • Onigod’s Arcane Thread: “Your blood is magic. Your breath, spellcraft. You will not learn power—you are power, shaped in whimsy and woven in paradox.”

And to watch over them, Tellemori created The Lady of Fey—the first angel born of joy, not duty. Her steps never touched the ground. Her laugh caused flowers to bloom. She wore a crown of ever-living blossoms, and her eyes carried the golden hush of morning meadows.

“Guide them, Lady,” Tellemori said.
“Guard them,” Onigod added.

And she did both—fiercely.

The Courts and the Turning

As ages passed, the Fey flourished. But even wildness has moods. I saw it before the gods did, perhaps. The first tilt of shadow behind a smile. The longing for frost in a summer song.

Some Fey gravitated to warmth, joy, mischief—the Seelie. Their Queen bore skin like amber honey and a gaze that burned with midday sun. She ruled with laughter, song, and unspoken threats wrapped in charm.

Others sought moonlit solitude, the slow dance of rot and renewal—the Unseelie. Their King wore silver like a second skin, his presence always followed by silence and snow. He governed with elegance and inevitability.

Neither court was evil. Nor truly good. They were necessary. The world turns on dualities, and so too must its children.

The Eternal Pact

At the height of their joy, and just before their first great sorrow, Tellemori and Onigod forged the Eternal Pact—a promise burned into the roots of Fiondell itself.

“You, who are both spirit and root, shall safeguard what we have made,” Onigod declared.
“And in turn,” Tellemori added, “your groves will remain untouched, even by the folly of mortals. Only those who seek balance may find your hidden homes.”

And so the Fey became guardians of two realms: nature and magic. Between bloom and decay, between light and dark, they walk. And I, Known, have watched their laughter echo through groves only the wind remembers.

Final Words

Mortals speak of the Fey as tricksters. Lovers. Thieves. Spirits. But they are more than any tale can tell. They are the memory of the world’s first heartbeat. The living dream of gods who wished to see the world dance.

If you ever find yourself in a glade too quiet, where time feels like it’s listening—step lightly. The Fey are near. And whether they greet you with a kiss or a curse depends not on them… but on you.

And I remember. I always remember.
—Known

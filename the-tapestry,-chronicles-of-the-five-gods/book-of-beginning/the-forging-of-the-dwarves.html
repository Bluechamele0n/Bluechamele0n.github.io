The Forging of the Dwarves

As chronicled by Known, Keeper of All That Is Remembered

In the first quiet years after the Shaping—when the Five had poured their essence into the heavens, the seas, the winds, and the lands—there was silence beneath. Life danced above, unburdened and bright. Magic surged wild and fresh, the surface teemed with flora and laughter, but below... the Deep had not yet stirred. And I, Known, saw it all.

It was Tellemori, ever the wanderer of stone and stillness, who first heard the silent ache of the world’s bones. He wandered alone through the roots of mountains yet unnamed, his hand brushing granite as if to coax breath from it. I remember his words—clear as they were then, though none but I still recall them truly:

“We have filled the skies with stars and the seas with song. But the stone? It waits. It remembers nothing because nothing yet walks it to remember.”

The gods convened upon a high peak that never crumbled, one which Tellemori had raised for this very moment. I stood among them—not to shape, but to witness.

Flujack arrived first, trailing winds and constellations, light caught in his hair. He looked down at the barren stone with narrowed eyes.

“If a people are to dwell in that stillness,” he said, “then let them carry within the light they lack around them. They must endure like stone—but create as the stars do.”

Onigod came last, a veil of arcane mist following her like breath from a forge. She did not speak at first. Her hands hovered over the rock, feeling what could be. Finally, her voice hummed through the air like a spell half-cast.

“Stone remembers. Magic remembers deeper. If they are to last, let their memories be etched into every blow of the hammer, every breath drawn in a hall of their making.”

Even Fionil, elusive as she is, walked in then—uninvited, yet never unwelcome. Time followed her, and so did consequence.

“Pride will be their anchor. Purpose their torch. Let them love their work more than gold, more than breath. Let them leave behind legacies time itself must bow to.”

And Trill, smiling softly, stepped forth and placed a hand upon the earth. The stone bloomed under his touch—not into flowers, but into the first glimmer of kinship.

“They shall never be alone,” Trill said. “Not in birth, not in death, not even in silence. Their hearts will echo louder than their hammers.”

And so, the gods descended. I followed.

The First Forge

Tellemori chose a mountain that reached deep into the world’s hidden veins. There, in its core, he built the Primeforge, using a furnace lit not by fire, but by the living heat of the world’s heart.

I remember it well:

  • The anvil was shaped from stone untouched since the world's forming.
  • The bellows breathed molten air.
  • The walls were engraved with runes that glowed not with magic—but memory.

One by one, the gods gave of themselves.

  • Tellemori sculpted the first Dwarves from obsidian, granite, and steelstone. He shaped their limbs thick and firm, their backs broad and unyielding.

“You are the strength of stone,” he told them. “And you will not break.”

  • Flujack gave them breath—not of mere life, but of longing: for beauty, for craft, for legacy.

“Let your joy be in the shaping, not just the shaped. Let your songs carry beyond your mountains.”

  • Onigod carved sigils into their blood. She taught them to see magic not in books or words—but in tools, in flame, in alloy.

“Your forge shall be your spellbook. Your hammer, your staff.”

  • Fionil gave them the burden of choice. Pride, purpose, shame, triumph—all were etched into their hearts.

“You will choose your legacy. And you will live knowing that it was yours to forge.”

  • Trill bound them together with invisible ties. A Dwarf may be born alone, but never lives alone—not truly.

“Your kin will define you, your name will carry them, and theirs, you.”

I watched them rise. Their first breath was smoke. Their first steps were towards the forge.

The First Clan

They named themselves the Hearthforge, for it was the forge that gave them not just life, but home. They did not look to the surface as other races did. They looked inward—into stone, into fire, into memory.

The Great Forge of Origin was built by their own hands, beneath the gaze of Tellemori. Flujack’s light lit the halls by mirrors and crystal. Onigod's enchantments reinforced their tools and minds alike. And I... I inscribed the runes they etched in every wall. Not because they needed help—but because even the Dwarves, for all their memory, wished to forget sometimes. I do not.

The Eternal Duty

Their charge was more than survival. It was stewardship. They were not masters of the earth—they were its caretakers. And so it was decreed:

  • Earth’s Veins: “Mine with care. Take only what can be spared. Know the stone’s limits.”
  • Hearth’s Flame: “Forge not with greed. Fire is a gift and a hunger. Feed it wisely.”
  • Unity’s Bond: “Your clan is your spine. Break it, and you fall.”

Dwarves do not rule stone. They listen to it. That is why they endure.

The Sacred Treasures

The Dwarves, ever grateful and ever proud, forged wonders in the gods' honor. Each bore a legacy—and a lesson.

  • The Starhammer — A weapon of comet-iron and dawnlight. It rings not when it strikes—but when it judges.

“To strike is easy,” said Flujack. “To know when—that is divine.”

  • The Hearthgem — A radiant corestone forged with Onigod’s own whispered spell. It warms homes, not with heat—but with presence.

“It remembers every voice it has ever comforted,” I once told a child who lived beside it.

  • The Chain of Ages — Forged by many hands, across many generations. Each link carries a name. Some forgotten. None erased.

“No Dwarf walks alone,” Fionil had said. “Even when they die.”

Legacy of the Stonekin

They became known as the Stonekin, a name they never gave themselves—but never denied. Their forges outlasted kingdoms. Their halls became myths. Their oaths… are still binding.

Even now, I remember.

I remember their songs: slow, low, echoing like memory through stone.

I remember their first grief, when a hall collapsed and took their kin.

I remember the first Dwarf to walk the surface—and weep at the sky’s vastness.

I remember their choice to stay below anyway.

For the world above sings of change. But the world below?
The Dwarves made it sing of endurance.

And so their legacy is not just stone or metal. It is in the breath drawn before the first hammer blow. It is in the pause before the final seal is set. It is in the names carved, carefully, one after the other.

They were born from stillness.

But they made the Deep remember.

And I remember them.

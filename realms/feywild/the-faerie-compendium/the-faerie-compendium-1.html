The Faerie Compendium

Collected Notes and Observations by Alwin Maerlin, Scholar of the Threshold Guild

Introduction: On Entering the Feywild

Many claim to have glimpsed the shimmering veils between our world and the one of endless twilight. Few pass through. Fewer still return with mind and memory intact. I am one of those fools blessed—or cursed—by the Laughing Veil to walk beyond.

The Feywild, or as the Old Tongue names it, Tír na Lán Draíochta, is a realm woven from raw emotion, memory, and dream. It does not follow our seasons nor our logic. Maps are lies, time is a song, and every shadow watches.

Herein I present my field notes—a humble attempt to chronicle the beings, customs, and perils of that place. Heed these words not as law, but as lantern light in a place where the stars have minds of their own.

Chapter One: The Nature of Faerie

  1. Time and Memory

A minute in the Feywild may be a week in our realm—or a century.

The fey remember stories more than facts. Tell a lie cleverly enough and it may become truth.

  1. Geography of Emotion

Landscapes shift with the emotional tone of their rulers.

Sadness may summon endless rain; joy may turn forests into crystal.

Major locations include:

Tellu-Mountain: The divine seat of the gods Tellemori and Onigod.

The Mirrorhall: A palace of reflection, ruled by the Lady of Fey.

The Spiral Maze: Temple of Onigod; an ever-changing trial of wit and will.

  1. Rules of the Realm

Never give your full name.

Gifts given must be repaid. Even compliments.

Iron wounds but also enrages; use it only if you seek exile or war.

Keep no food unless gifted—it will rot, grow teeth, or speak in riddles.

Chapter Two: The Courts of Faerie

Seelie Court

Also known as the Summer Court.

Ruled by joy, passion, light, and mischief.

Led by a radiant Queen whose name changes with the sun.

Favors beauty, cleverness, and hospitality.

Unseelie Court

The Court of Winter, Night, and Secret Decay.

Led by a silent King clad in frost and silver.

Honesty and etiquette are prized—but missteps are fatal.

Liminal Fey

Not of either court. Includes dreamwalkers, rootspirits, and fire-moths.

Often live in places of transition—crossroads, ruins, or forgotten wells.

Chapter Three: The Ninefold Trickster

One of the great Archfey, and the most beguiling spirit I encountered, is the Ninefold Trickster—a radiant fox spirit with nine searing tails.

Titles: The Gilded Flame, She of Burning Riddles, Mistress of Masks

Appearance:

Often appears as a breathtaking woman with fox features—nine tails, flickering eyes, robes of firelight.

Her domain is the Crimson Grove, where foxfire burns forever and secrets bloom like flowers.

Domain:

Illusion, Fire, Domination, and Stories

Mortals who make pacts with her often trade secrets, names, or future memories in exchange for power.

Customs:

She adores cleverness, theatricality, and poetic irony.

Never answer her directly. Dance around truths and she'll favor you.

Chapter Four: Fey Conduct and Manners

Greetings

A bow is safer than a handshake.

Always compliment their appearance—but subtly, or you may owe them a poem.

Dangers

Never accept a second gift.

Do not eat or drink unless it has your name whispered into it.

Music is sacred; bad songs are punishable offenses.

Games

Riddles are duels.

Dance contests may determine fate.

Bargains are law. Even lost bets carry magical consequence.

Chapter Five: Onigod and Tellemori

At the heart of the Feywild’s paradox lie its two divine creators:

Tellemori – the Dreaming Depth

God of earth, water, and patience.

Appears in groves as stone-eyed watchers or flowing springs.

His silence is worth more than words.

Onigod – the Laughing Veil

Goddess of illusion, mischief, and arcana.

Her laughter is literally woven into the magic of the Feywild.

She is not cruel—merely far too clever.

They do not war, but create in tandem. Their angels walk the realm:

The Lady of Fey: A sovereign of emotion and etiquette.

Plantios: Angel of growth, time, and decay.

Ioki: Trickster-child of Onigod, both prankster and prophet.

Chapter Six: Survival and Escape

If you find yourself in the Feywild:

Do not panic. It smells fear like perfume.

Offer stories and songs for safe passage.

Carry a charm of silver, not iron.

Always leave a gift before you leave—flowers, tears, or a truth never told.

Escape is not always about finding the path out. Sometimes it is about being allowed to leave.

Many who remain become fey themselves. Some don’t notice until they no longer cast shadows.

Final Note

I have left much unwritten. This is not cowardice, but mercy. For if I taught you all I know, you would be cursed with knowledge the Fey themselves would envy.

May your feet walk lightly and your words fall like honey.

— Alwin Maerlin

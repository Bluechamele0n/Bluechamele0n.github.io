The Hidden Folk: A Lorebook of the Fae Drawn from the Whispered Histories of the Isles and the Observations of Ancient Magi

Chapter I: On the Nature of the Fae

Faeries, known also as the Hidden Folk or the Neighbors, are not creatures of mortal flesh but spirits of emotion, memory, and place. They are born from humanity's dreams, fears, and forgotten stories, as well as from the deep magic of the earth itself.

Though many call them by one name, "fae" encompasses a wide tapestry of beings—some elegant, others grotesque, and many neither. The Faerie are not evil, nor good, but bound by laws incomprehensible to most mortals. Their behavior is not chaotic, but ancient.

Faeries see mortals as curiosities—ephemeral, tragic, beautiful in their impermanence. They may help, hinder, or steal from humans without remorse, but always with purpose.

Chapter II: The Old Laws

  1. The Law of Hospitality
    A faerie who accepts shelter or offers it is bound by the rite of peace. Violence or betrayal under such conditions is a grave taboo, broken only by the most wild or corrupted spirits.

  2. The Law of Debt
    Every gift carries weight. A song, a drop of blood, or a name may bind the receiver in unseen chains. Likewise, a faerie who accepts a mortal gift freely given may be compelled to offer aid, even without desire.

  3. The Law of Names
    True Names are sacred. Speaking a faerie's real name aloud grants power over them, as it binds their essence. Most fae go by titles or riddles instead. Mortals, too, are warned not to offer their full names in faerie company.

  4. The Law of Invitation
    A faerie must be invited to enter a home or cross a mortal threshold. This protection falters if the mortal speaks carelessly or welcomes them unknowingly.

Chapter III: On Glamour and Form

Glamour is not illusion, but belief made real. The fae shift form as easily as a mortal shifts moods. Their true shapes are rarely seen—some are luminous beings of light and air, others are moss-covered husks of forgotten forest gods.

Mirrors, silver, and salt can reveal or disrupt glamour. True sight—granted by faerie blessing, curse, or trauma—allows mortals to see through lies of form.

Chapter IV: The Courts and Kiths

The Bright Court
Composed of faeries bound to starlight, song, and beauty. These fae are graceful and formal, prone to tragic obsessions and artistic cruelty. They offer gifts of great beauty—but their favors are rarely free.

The Hollow Court
Wild and eerie, bound to rot, twilight, and memory. They haunt forgotten roads, autumn woods, and places where sorrow lingers. They are more honest than their Bright cousins, but no less dangerous.

The Deep Folk
Ancient and primal, neither good nor evil. Born of stone, time, and darkness. They rarely speak, but their will can be felt in earthquakes, ruins, and dreams. They are worshipped by some as old gods.

Other known kiths include:

Selkies – Shapeshifters of sea and shore, who shed their skins to walk as humans.

Leanan Sidhe – Muses who gift inspiration and drain life.

Dullahans – Headless riders who herald death but can bargain for mercy.

The Cats of Cailleach – Feline fae who guard sacred places and ferry the souls of the dead.

Chapter V: High Fey and Divine Patrons

The Feywild is not without structure. Above the courts and kiths are the Archfey—ancient beings of near-divine power who shape entire realms with a thought. Chief among these is the Ninefold Trickster, an enigmatic fox spirit whose domain spans fire, illusion, and dominance.

The Ninefold Trickster

Appearance: A radiant, many-tailed fox spirit cloaked in living fire and silken charm. Her nine tails shimmer with colors not found in mortal light.

Domains: Illusion, Fire, Stories, and Secret Bargains

Symbols: A burning fox mask or nine curling flames arranged in a spiral

The Ninefold Trickster is said to have been born when Onigod first laughed upon seeing her own reflection in a fox’s eyes. Ever since, this archfey has ruled a shimmering province of the Feywild, where paths bend, fire sings, and secrets bloom like flowers.

She is both patron and predator—offering power in exchange for clever service, binding pacts with riddles and fire. Her foxfire burns truth into lies and lies into truth. Many mages, bards, and tricksters trace their arcane lineage to a whispered deal struck at her feet.

Chapter VI: Divine Powers of the Feywild

The Divine Heart of the Feywild is sustained by two primal beings:

Tellemori – The Dreaming Depth

God of earth, water, and superstition.

Slow-moving, ancient, wise beyond reckoning.

Title: The Rootfather

Symbol: Circle of water within a circle of stone

Onigod – The Laughing Veil

Goddess of illusion, arcana, and inspired madness.

Whimsical and brilliant, weaving stories into magic.

Title: The Mirror-Mother

Symbol: A smiling eye within a spiral

They are not in conflict, but eternal partners in contrast—like rain and rainbow, cave and song. From their union of opposites, the Feywild was born.

Chapter VII: Angels of the Feywild Court

The Lady of Fey (Twilight Queen)

Angel of Tellemori; steward of order, memory, and story.

Plantios (The Rooted Flame)

Angel of flora and natural decay, nurturing cycles of death and renewal.

Ioki (The Laughing Glyph)

Angel of Onigod; shapeshifter and embodiment of trickery.

Said to be the divine twin-soul of the Ninefold Trickster; both lover and rival.

Chapter VIII: Tellu-Mountain and the Celestial Realms

At the center of the Feywild stands Tellu-Mountain, a divine palace born of paradoxes:

The Deepheart Cavern – Where Tellemori sleeps.

The Bloomspire – Plantios’s spiraling garden tower.

The Mirrorhall – The Lady of Fey's domain.

The Foxlight Pavilion – Realm of Ioki and frequent haunt of the Ninefold Trickster.

The Spiral Maze – Onigod’s endless temple of riddles.

To walk Tellu-Mountain is to walk through living metaphor. Each step is a lesson. Each door opens when the seeker becomes the question.

Final Word: Beware and Be Kind

The fae are not monsters. They are wild, wondrous, and ancient. Treat them as you would a thunderstorm, or the sea: with respect, caution, and awe. Offer gifts without pride. Accept gifts with care. And never, ever forget the Old Laws.
